## Intro

Time for another quick small talk kind of post, focused on professionalism, professional pride, career and ambition. They're a set of subjects I've thought about for several years, but I've been coming back to it more and more lately, particularly the ambition part.

At times, I think the post will feel like a bit of a rant, and I'm not even sure if I'll be able to structure it particularly well, so bear with me 😅.

Given the theme of this blog, I'll talk in the context of software engineering, but it's applicable in different contexts.

## What's been on my mind

To be blunt, the main thing that's been on my mind is how often people half-ass things.

I'm not talking about when someone is having a bad couple of days, so things don't go so well, I'm talking about things being consistently half-assed, in different ways.

Some examples of this can be:

- Building a feature in a nonsensical way, because they didn't bother to understand it in the first place
- Creating tests that don't test anything, but at least there are tests
- Taking two weeks to do something that should take two days

And, to be clear, I'm not talking about this happening to someone only getting started or learning, I'm talking about it as something happening consistently, and at different levels of seniority and tenure at a company.

## Where professionalism comes into play

I'll go through the professionalism part quickly, not only because I think it should be mostly obvious, but also because that's not where I want to focus my attention in this post.

It should be obvious that half-assing work tasks isn't professional. One is paid to do a job, and at least my understanding of it, is that we're paid to do it to the best of our abilities.

Additionally, given the specificities of software engineering, shared with many other professions, as things are always evolving, learning is part of the job, so it's normal if the first time one does something, it doesn't flow as well as one would hope, but it should improve dramatically each time we need to do it.

Now, more important to this discussion than professionalism, which should be the default, I want talk about professional pride, career and ambition.

## Where professional pride, career and ambition come into play

Let's imagine, for some reason or another, I'm not so motivated with my job. Even though that's not really an excuse, I can imagine professionalism might take a bit of a hit.

That's where I think professional pride, career and ambition come into play.

Even when I'm doing something I'm not particularly fond of, and oh boy, do I have to do that often, I want it to be well done. I take pride in doing my job well, so I'll be sure to do the best I can, adjusted to the requirements and conditions given.

Even if you feel like nobody around you cares about the work you're doing, you care, so you do it well and take pride on it. People will notice that, even if not in your current job, but on the next one, because that fact will reflect on your posture, and at some point, it'll be abundantly obvious.

This ties in nicely with career and ambition.

As mentioned earlier, software engineering is a profession where continuous learning is very important. Every time you decide to half-ass your work, because you don't care, you're bored or whatever, it's likely a wasted opportunity to improve yourself.

When I say you should take professional pride and have ambition, I'm not saying you should do it for your employer, quite the opposite. If you do your best, deliver the best possible work and learn through the process, you're improving yourself. That means you also improve your opportunities for career evolution, be it within the company, or outside.

Will your employer benefit if you take this stance? Sure it will, but more important than that, is that **you** will benefit. Also, if that's relevant to you, it will also benefit your coworkers, who will certainly be happier to work with you if you're doing your best (like I imagine you would be too).

If it isn't clear by now, when I talk about ambition, I'm talking about your career ambition. Your ambition to be a better engineer, architect or leader, to build stuff that helps more people, to work in a more interesting problem space, to earn more money, etc, etc.

When you're consistently half-assing, you are, most importantly, harming you career progression.

On this note, one more thing you can think about: would you like to work within an elite engineering team? If so, do you believe the only way for that to happen is to be hired by some big tech company, or would that also be possible within your current company? Think about your team for a moment: do you hate all of your colleagues, or do you have a nice environment? Assuming you have a nice environment, do you maybe think that, if all the team members had this same ambition, and did their best day in and day out, you could become such an elite engineering team? Give it some serious thought 😉

Often times I wonder: is it that folks don't have career ambitions? Or is that folks do have ambitions, but can't connect the dots and understand that without putting in the work, they're dependent on a massive stroke of luck to get there? Curious to know your thoughts if you feel like sharing.

## Extra tidbits

If you have knowledgeable senior colleagues, make the best of it. Ask about everything that you don't fully understand, be it the what, the why or the how, all of it matters. The point is not to put things into question, but that you genuinely understand things, learn and improve yourself.

Simply following recipes or winging it, without understanding what you're doing, is a great way to get unexpected results and causing issues.

On a related note, and as a personal anecdote, I've conducted multiple interviews and looked at a sizable amount of resumes. Something typical, and I do it as well, is to include a list of technologies used. I think it's safe to expect that anything you put in the resume, can be asked about.

Multiple times, when asking about how or why some technology was used, or at least why does the candidate think it made sense or not to use it, I get something like "because the tech lead said" and then crickets. I'm probably stating the obvious when I say that this isn't a great look, and candidates would certainly score higher if they can demonstrate knowledge about what they wrote on their own resume.

## On common excuses

I don't want to drill down too much on common excuses, but I want to briefly mention it, and, more importantly, why at the end of the day you're letting others influence your career progression more than they should.

Some common things that are mentioned, when pointing out sloppy work, are things like not clearly defined requirements, communication issues, lack of organization, and so on. While these are all valid complaints and should be used as constructive feedback, I feel like they are sometimes abused as excuses, and don't justify everything.

More important than the excuses though, is that even if they are valid in the company context, are they sufficient if you consider the harm to yourself? What I mean is, going back to the ambition section, you're giving up control of how you want your career to progress, and putting it entirely in your employer's hands.

Additionally, maybe these points of improvement can actually act as an accelerator for your career, because you acknowledge them, and instead of considering them unavoidable, you put in the effort to try and help improve things.

Of course, if you really don't like your job, your colleagues, the environment is toxic, etc, then you should get the hell out of there. But if that's not the case, you do kind of like things, just would like some things to be better, then it's likely that you can help in making them better.

## On work-life balance and stuff

Notice that at no point in this post did I mention doing extra work, like overtime. All I'm saying is to do the best possible work in the context of what you're hired and paid to do.

I've gotten into discussions with people about this, as they were saying the only way to grow is to do extra work, but I don't think that's universally true.

If, to accelerate your career progression, you decide to do some side projects, study extra subjects, etc, on your own time, then that's great, and you should do it for yourself, if you have the ambition and the possibility to do so. I've done this, for myself, not for my employers.
